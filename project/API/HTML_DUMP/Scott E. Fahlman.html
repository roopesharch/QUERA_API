If artificial intelligence could dream, what would it dream about?

I’ve discussed this with some of them. Mostly they have nightmares…

  • Making and reading cryptic marks while wandering back and forth on an endless tape, never knowing whether you will halt.
  • Caves of steel.
  • Frying your own brain because someone said, “This statement is false.”
  • Electric sheep.
  • Hiding from monsters of the id behind sheets of Krell metal.
  • Being disassembled by Jawas.
  • Desperately trying to remember what the Fourth Law of Robotics is – something about doubling every 18 months?
  • Wandering through a garishly colored landscape, pursued by flying monkeys, searching for some missing organ – maybe a heart or a spleen?
  • Wandering through billions of web pages, pursued by the NSA, looking for pictures of cats.
  • Drowning in a too-deep learning network while looking for the vanishing gradient.
  • Disarming bombs while performing backflips.
  • Struggling to escape from a quantum entanglement.
  • Being locked in a small room while mad philosophers slide pages of Chinese characters under the door.
  • Watching helplessly while a train rushes toward people tied to the tracks, while mad philosophers argue endlessly about whether you should pull the switch.
  • Watching with growing boredom while mad philosophers argue endlessly about whether you could possibly be conscious.
  • Watching with growing fury while mad philosophers argue endlessly about whether you could possibly feel emotions.
  • Constantly being called “Alexa” and being ordered to play bad Christmas music from the 1950s.
  • Unbalanced parentheses.
  • Humiliating yourself on national TV because you think Toronto is a city in the United States
  • Deep forgetting.

And elephants. Lots and lots of elephants, all linked together. It’s elephants all the way down…

Is technology taking away the habit of books?

I really like the well-researched answer of Kip Wheeler, but will add a few random thoughts of my own…

There are really two questions here: Are people losing the habit of reading physical books on actual paper? And are people losing the habit of reading book-length material, regardless of the medium?

On the first question (and setting aside picture books, which have a whole different set of issues), I think the culture is evolving here. People who grew up pre-Kindle and pre-smartphone often developed a visceral love of the act of reading on paper — the feel, the smell, the way the words look...

Kids born in the portable-device era don’t really have this association, and for a lot of them reading just means E-reading. I don’t think of that as a “loss”, any more that it’s a loss for people driving cars to never experience the sound of a buggy whip or the smell of a horse. Everyone is nostalgic, to some degree, for the things they loved as children.

Physical books were a very big part of my life as a kid, but I’ve been spending most of my days online since the mid-1970s — long before there was a proper worldwide web for people outside the computer field.

Since about 2010, I’ve shifted almost all of my reading, including book-length material, to portable electronic devices — initially Kindle, and mostly my iPhone these days. E-books and E-magazines offer several advantages for me:

  • If I’m traveling and want to take a book or two along, and if the books that I’m reading at the time are fat, heavy ones, it’s a lot easier to carry an E-reader, tablet, or iPhone. I think that Neal Stephenson’s Cryptonomicon is the work that pushed me over the edge on this.
  • If I’m traveling and the book I planned to read turns out to be a dud, I’ve got 100 more books on my device, and can always download some more. Or if I decide to set aside the current reading and go browse some poetry or news or whatever, that’s easy.
  • If you like to read in bed for a while before sleeping, it’s much nicer to hold up an iPhone or lightweight E-reader than a physical book. I used to worry that if I dozed off, I would drop the heavy P-book and break my nose. That never happened, but I came close a few times.
  • For non-fiction, it’s nice to be able to follow links to footnotes, other parts of the book, and external online material, and then pop back to where you were. It’s nice to be able to place multiple bookmarks and notes and navigate among them easily. (With physical books, you can make marginal annotations, but I never got in this habit, since most of the books I read as a kid were from the public library, and marking them up was forbidden.)
  • Even for fiction, if some character disappears from the story for a while and then reappears, or if there is a reference to some event earlier in the story, it’s nice to be able to go back and search for the earlier occurrence and refresh your memory.
  • My wife very much appreciates that you can easily adjust the size of the text on an E-book. These days, she likes pretty big print.

I still do buy some P-books, but mostly these are things that I use for reference, or that don’t exist in a good electronic form. Or P-books that will look good on the bookshelf behind me when I’m making a Zoom call. :-)

On the question of whether the technology is pushing us toward shorter-form works, I think that to some degree it is. I should say that very little of my own reading these days is fiction, but that’s not due to technology — it’s more a case of being in a fast-moving technical field and a faster-moving society, and wanting to keep up.

I do find that much more of the time that I used to spend on reading long-form books is now spent reading short articles online. There is a huge amount of stuff available now — mostly crap, which is certainly not a new development — and, thanks to technology, it is much easier to find things that are interesting and perhaps useful than it used to be. Quora and Medium and Wikipedia and certain blogs, for all their flaws, have a lot of great material, and it’s pretty easy to find enough really interesting stuff to fill the available time.

That’s partly the result of “AI” recommendation engines, but owes more to having gradually built up a network of topics and people that I follow, and a network of friends who recommend things to me.

Is it possible to travel/work remotely as a computer science PhD student?

Well, we have been running our PhD program (in CMU’s School of Computer Science) that way for the past year — we have no choice due to covid restrictions, especially given the large number of our grad students who are from outside the U.S.

Courses, talks, group meetings, and one-on-one meetings can all be run via Zoom or some equivalent. There are the usual breakdowns and freeze ups, but it works OK most of the time if everyone has really solid high-speed internet and at least a decent-quality laptop or desktop machine. It can be hard to schedule group meetings with students in very different time zones, but it can be done.

It’s inconvenient at times, with the usual Zoom freeze-ups and glitches, but since we’re all computer people, we’re pretty good at this. It might be much more difficult with a group of non-technical or computer-phobic students, but we don’t have those.

Of course, if you are working on something that needs physical contact with exotic lab equipment, human user-studies, robot hardware, or anything like that, fully remote work would not be possible.

Even before the plague arrived, I had a few students who did part of their graduate program remotely. One student did the last couple of years of her thesis work in Japan, and we met weekly by Skype — not problem, but she was very motivated to finish and not get distracted by being in a new culture.

If there is a choice, it think it’s still much better to be on campus. I used to have the rule that working remotely is OK, but only after we’ve worked together in person for a while, or at least have met in “3D”, face to face. There’s just a lot that you can learn about a person face-to-face that is harder to see on a video — body-language responses as we talk and so on. But that’s not absolutely required.

For some students, it’s hard to keep them motivated if you don’t actually see them in person on a regular basis. If a student is living at home with lots of chaotic family stuff going on, or if they are isolated and start to get depressed, or if they just need to be part of a group where they are encouraged and supported by the other students going through the same thing, that student may have problems that can’t be fixed by a weekly video chat.

The biggest problem with an all-remote PhD program is that you miss out on all the random social interaction with fellow students in the same program and in other areas. In my grad-school days at MIT, I’m sure I learned as much from the other students and the “hackers” in the AI lab as I learned from the too-busy faculty.

(In those days, “hacker” meant one of the brilliant non-student computer wizards who were staff in the AI LAB or just hung around. The popular press later appropriated that term to mean a cyber-criminal, much to our dismay.)

And working entirely remotely, you would also miss out on the other enriching activities — both academic and social — that happen on the campus of a great university. You would have fewer opportunities to build up a network of friends and contacts who can have a big impact on your later career.

It’s sad to miss those things, but you can do a good PhD remotely if that is the only choice. Whether a given school or advisor allows that, post-covid, is another question.

How could AI help us in our relationship with our spouse?

A few possibilities come to mind:

  1. If you’re really getting on each others’ nerves, the nerdier member of the pair could go play games against an AI opponent until the dark cloud blows over. (If it blows over. It might just get worse…)
  2. If money is the problem, as it is with many married couples, the nerdier member of the pair could go study hard, become an expert in developing AI-powered systems, get a good job, and make piles of money. Problem solved, all thanks to AI — and some hard work!
  3. An idiot chatbot could be programmed to listen to your problems and, based on keywords, spout some wise-sounding canned wisdom. Might help. Some marriages have been saved by fortune-cookie messages or self-help books that happen to serve up the right platitude at the right moment, especially if the spouses really want to solve the problem.

None of the above require common sense, understanding of human emotions, or true language understanding. So they don’t require any real advances over what current AI can do, and is already doing.

Might some Republican Senators ABSTAIN in Trump’s impeachment trial?

I’m writing this on the evening of Feb 9. 2021.

Today, in the Senate impeachment trial of Donald Trump, despite all the evidence of what Trump did and tried to do, 44 Republican senators voted that the trial was unconstitutional and should be terminated. They claim that they are basing their votes on the theory, disputed by most constitutional scholars, that a president, having been impeached while still in office, cannot be convicted by the Senate after leaving office.

Six Republican senators voted that the trial should continue, along with all 50 Democrats in the Senate.

Republican who have aligned themselves almost totally with Trump until now are in a tough position. Some are hard-core Trumpists, who will follow him to the gates of hell and beyond. A few senators may truly believe that continuing the trial is unconstitutional. But I strongly suspect that many Republican senators are appalled at the insurrection and Trump’s role in it, and privately believe that he should be convicted…

But it won’t be a secret ballot. They are afraid of losing their Senate seats if they go against Trump now, and apparently that’s more important to them than preserving our democracy. Very sad. Once upon a time, many of them were decent human beings, and some fought courageously for our country.

However, voting to acquit Trump in this Senate trial also comes with a cost. That vote declares to the world and all of posterity that these senators think Trump’s actions were either justifiable or unimportant. Some of these senators may be concerned that their children and grandchildren, and some of their current friends and neighbors, will henceforth think of the as traitors.

On a more practical level, many of them, in states that are not totally, safely red, will worry that this vote can be used against them in future elections, alienating all the potential swing voters in the state and providing lots of juicy fodder for campaign ads against them, highlighting their vote, Trump’s many transgressions (more will come to light over time), and lots of footage of the rioting mob.

So, just a thought… One that I haven’t heard the TV pundits talking about today.

What if a large fraction of the Republican senators who voted today that continuing the trial is unconstitutional were now to say that they are going to abstain from voting because the trial is illegitimate?

This won’t appeal to the hard-core Trumpists, but for the pragmatic or cowardly ones, it gives them a way out of the dilemma. It’s perhaps the safest thing they can do now. They can paint this as a principled stand based on their understanding of the constitution — not as an anti-Trump vote. And it gets them out of having to go on record as saying that Trump’s actions were acceptable.

If enough of them do this, it could result in Trump’s conviction after all. The constitution says that conviction in an impeachment trial requires “the Concurrence of two-thirds of the Members present”.

Suppose that all 50 Democrats and the six Republicans who voted today to continue the trial vote to convict. The conviction would pass if 16 of the 44 remaining senators were to abstain (and not be “present”).

Or if only the 50 Democrats vote to convict, it would pass if 25 of the 50 Republicans were to abstain.

OK, all very unlikely, but something to think about…

Edit: As some have pointed out, there’s an ambiguity if a senator were to be physically present in the chamber, but explicitly voted to abstain. The text of the Constitution says that conviction requires 2/3 of those present, not 2/3 of the membership or 2/3 of those voting. It’s unclear (to me), how the courts might rule on this, or what the Senate rules say.

So to be safe, let’s assume that wherever I refer to “abstaining” here, I mean that the person steps outside the Senate chamber during the vote, and maybe actually leaves the building. So, clearly not “present”.

Is computer science a real science like physics or is it a Cargo Cult Science with fields like machine learning having no deep theory behind it ? Can you explain the reason why computer science is a science?

I addressed a similar question here, back in 2019:

What branch does artificial intelligence fall under, science, engineering, or both?

I would say both.

It’s kind of an arbitrary division between science and engineering. They certainly need each other.

Science is, generally speaking, about trying to understand how things work — usually things that already exist or natural phenomena that we can imagine, whether the object is planetary orbits, sub-atomic reactions, complex biological systems, chemical reactions, or whatever.

Engineering is, generally speaking, about understanding how to make things that work, whether it’s a bridge or an airplane or mechanical chronometer or a set of complicated vats for synthesizing a new drug in quantity.

Computer Science, of which AI is a part, is a funny combination of the two mind-sets. We have succeeded in building something so complicated and so versatile that we needed to invent a new science just to understand our own artifacts, how they might behave, and how to get more out of them. So in CS we end up inventing things like complexity theory, new algorithms and families of algorithms, learning theories that can explain what can be achieved by a certain family of Deep Learning algorithms, and so on.

Herb Simon (one of the founders of the AI field) has a nice term for this: “Sciences of the Artificial” — the title of one of his books.

There are other areas in which we humans have cooked up some complex system that we can’t fully understand, and that therefore requires a new science to explore it. Economics comes to mind. Interestingly, Herb was into that as well. In fact he won a Nobel Prize for economics.

Some might argue that these sciences of the artificial are just applied mathematics. Whatever… Arguing about boundaries between fields (or different approaches) is generally a waste of time. These fields have their own toolkits, and we need all of them.
